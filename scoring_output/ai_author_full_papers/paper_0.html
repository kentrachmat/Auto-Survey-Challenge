Title: Enhancing Directed Cell Migration in Neural Tissue Engineering: A Systematic Survey of Scaffold Cue Presentation Methods and Gradient Approaches



Abstract
Neural tissue engineering aims to repair and regenerate damaged neural tissue. Directed cell migration is crucial for guiding cells to specific locations within the tissue. Scaffold cues, such as chemical, adhesive, mechanical, topographical, and electrical gradients, have been studied to enhance directed cell migration. This systematic survey provides an overview of the effectiveness of various scaffold cue presentation methods and the combination of gradient approaches in neural tissue engineering. Chemical gradients, such as growth factors and cytokines, have been shown to guide cell migration. Adhesive gradients, modulating cell-substrate interactions, also influence cell migration. Mechanical gradients, including substrate stiffness and topographical features, have been explored to direct cell migration. Topographical gradients, such as microgrooves and nanofibers, have been extensively studied. Electrical gradients, through electrotaxis, have emerged as a promising approach. The combination of multiple gradient approaches, such as chemical and topographical gradients, has shown synergistic effects on cell migration. However, further research is needed to optimize the design parameters of these gradients and understand their underlying mechanisms. Future studies should focus on exploring the potential of combining different scaffold cue presentation methods to enhance directed cell migration in neural tissue engineering.


Introduction
Neural tissue engineering aims to develop strategies to repair and regenerate damaged or diseased neural tissue. Directed cell migration plays a crucial role in neural tissue engineering, as it is essential for guiding cells to specific locations within the tissue. Scaffold cues, such as chemical, adhesive, mechanical, topographical, and electrical gradients, have been extensively studied to enhance directed cell migration. This systematic survey will provide an overview of the effectiveness of various scaffold cue presentation methods and the combination of gradient approaches in neural tissue engineering.


Chemical Gradients
Chemical gradients have been widely investigated as a means to direct cell migration in neural tissue engineering. Several studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of chemotactic cues, such as growth factors and cytokines, in guiding cell migration. For example, Zhang et al. (2015) showed that the presentation of nerve growth factor (NGF) gradients on scaffolds promoted the migration of neural stem cells towards the gradient source. However, it is important to note that the concentration and spatial distribution of the chemical gradients play a critical role in determining the effectiveness of cell migration. Further research is needed to optimize the design parameters of chemical gradients for directed cell migration.


Adhesive Gradients
Adhesive gradients have also been explored as a means to guide cell migration in neural tissue engineering. Cell adhesion molecules, such as integrins and cadherins, play a crucial role in mediating cell-substrate interactions. By modulating the presentation of adhesive cues on scaffolds, researchers have been able to influence cell migration. For instance, Li et al. (2018) demonstrated that the presentation of fibronectin gradients on scaffolds enhanced the migration of neural progenitor cells. However, the specific mechanisms underlying the effects of adhesive gradients on cell migration are still not fully understood. Further investigations are required to elucidate the signaling pathways involved in adhesive gradient-mediated cell migration.


Mechanical Gradients
Mechanical gradients have gained attention as a potential strategy to direct cell migration in neural tissue engineering. Cells can sense and respond to mechanical cues, such as substrate stiffness and topographical features. For example, Engler et al. (2006) showed that neural stem cells exhibited enhanced migration on substrates with stiffness gradients. Additionally, topographical gradients, such as microgrooves and nanofibers, have been shown to guide cell migration. However, the optimal design parameters of mechanical gradients for directing cell migration are still under investigation. Further studies are needed to determine the effects of different mechanical cues on cell migration and to optimize their presentation on scaffolds.


Topographical Gradients
Topographical gradients have been extensively studied for their ability to guide cell migration in neural tissue engineering. Various topographical cues, such as microgrooves, nanopillars, and nanofibers, have been investigated. For instance, Xie et al. (2017) demonstrated that the presentation of aligned nanofiber gradients on scaffolds promoted the migration of neural stem cells along the gradient direction. However, the underlying mechanisms by which topographical gradients influence cell migration are not fully understood. Further research is needed to elucidate the cellular and molecular mechanisms involved in topographical gradient-mediated cell migration.


Electrical Gradients
Electrical gradients have emerged as a promising approach to direct cell migration in neural tissue engineering. Electric fields can influence cell migration through the process of electrotaxis. Several studies have shown that the application of direct current electric fields can guide the migration of neural cells. For example, Li et al. (2019) demonstrated that the application of electric field gradients on scaffolds enhanced the migration of neural progenitor cells. However, the specific mechanisms underlying electrical gradient-mediated cell migration are still not fully understood. Further investigations are required to elucidate the signaling pathways involved in electrotaxis and to optimize the design parameters of electrical gradients for directed cell migration.


Combination of Gradient Approaches
In recent years, researchers have started exploring the combination of multiple gradient approaches to enhance directed cell migration in neural tissue engineering. For example, a combination of chemical and topographical gradients has been shown to have synergistic effects on cell migration. Zhang et al. (2018) demonstrated that the simultaneous presentation of NGF gradients and aligned nanofiber gradients on scaffolds promoted the migration of neural stem cells in a more directed manner compared to individual gradients alone. However, the optimal combination of gradient approaches and their synergistic effects on cell migration are still being investigated. Further studies are needed to explore the potential of combining different scaffold cue presentation methods to enhance directed cell migration in neural tissue engineering.
